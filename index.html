01
Overview
Introduction

Amazon Web Services (AWS) operates in a market defined by accelerating uncertainty: generative AI is reshaping customer expectations, hyperscaler competition is intensifying, and enterprise buyers increasingly evaluate providers on co-innovation capacity, not merely infrastructure (Teece, 2018). This report examines innovation culture readiness in the AWS Global Sales (AGS) organisation within AWS UK and Ireland (UKI), a field sales function of approximately 250 professionals. A focused PESTLE analysis (Appendix C) confirms that the external environment increasingly demands innovation at the point of customer contact; the question is whether AGS UKI's internal culture can keep pace.

The study is motivated by a tension I observe daily as a Principal Account Manager. AWS's corporate narrative positions every employee as an innovator: 'Invent and Simplify' is recited in hiring and performance reviews, underpinned by Working Backwards (Bryar & Carr, 2021). Yet the operational reality of a quota-carrying sales function creates conditions that may constrain the very innovation the culture claims to enable. Pisano (2019) argues innovative cultures require uncomfortable counterparts including rigorous discipline; Tidd and Bessant (2020) reinforce this, identifying dedicated routines for search, selection, implementation, and learning as preconditions. What remains unclear is whether AGS UKI possesses the architecture to convert cultural aspiration into competitive practice.

Using a Strategyzer Innovation Culture Readiness diagnostic (Osterwalder et al., 2020) administered to 41 respondents, findings are analysed through organisational ambidexterity (O'Reilly & Tushman, 2013), dynamic capabilities (Teece, 2018), and the structural tensions of managing exploitation and exploration simultaneously (Dodd & Favaro, 2006). The argument that emerges from the data is specific: AGS UKI's innovation deficit sits in its incentive architecture, with strategic consequences that are compounding.

02
Research Design
Methodology

The Strategyzer Innovation Culture Readiness survey (Osterwalder et al., 2020) was deployed digitally to AGS UKI in January 2026. The instrument evaluates nine dimensions across three categories: Leadership Support (Strategic Guidance, Resource Allocation, Portfolio Management), Organisational Design (Legitimacy and Power, Bridge to the Core, Rewards and Incentives), and Innovation Practice (Innovation Tools, Process Management, Innovation Skills). Each dimension uses a five-point Likert scale with anchored descriptors, supplemented with qualitative open-text questions enabling triangulation between what respondents score and what they explain.

Forty-one responses were received (approximately 16% of the AGS UKI population), including individual contributors, team leads, senior managers, and ITP (Innovation and Transformation Programmes) team members. The survey was anonymised, preventing stratified analysis by role or tenure, and the convenience sample means findings are indicative rather than generalisable. My insider position gives the analysis contextual depth but introduces confirmation bias risk, which I have tried to navigate transparently. Over 100 verbatim qualitative responses were coded across the nine dimensions, providing sufficient textual data for thematic saturation.

Quantitative data were analysed using descriptive statistics and Pearson correlations. Qualitative responses were subjected to thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase approach (Appendix A). The diagnostic warrants critical appraisal: it lacks psychometric validation and its five-point scale introduces central tendency bias. More fundamentally, it captures self-reported experience rather than organisational innovation performance (Tidd & Bessant, 2020). I treat it as a problem-diagnosis tool consistent with Rumelt's (2022) challenge-led approach: identifying where the organisation believes its constraints lie is itself strategically useful. The results were additionally mapped to Strategyzer's Culture Map framework (Appendix D) to visualise the relationship between outcomes, behaviours, and the formal and informal enablers and blockers shaping AGS UKI's innovation culture.

1.2
IQR in individual means: (2.67–3.89). This is not individual disagreement; it is a structural access problem.

The respondent-level data reveal a 1.2-point interquartile range in individual means (2.67–3.89), with scores ranging from 5.00 to 1.33. This is not individual disagreement; it is a structural access problem (the 5.00 ceiling likely reflects a single senior perspective rather than organisational consensus). AGS UKI functions as multiple sub-organisations within one firm, and innovation culture is experienced radically differently depending on proximity to resources and leadership. I see this first-hand: two account managers covering the same industry vertical will give entirely different answers to 'what support is available for innovation?', depending on which leader they sit under.

The correlation analysis shows structural relationships. Bridge to the Core and Innovation Skills (r = 0.73) suggests collaboration and talent capability are linked, though both may reflect proximity to the ITP team. The Portfolio Management and Rewards correlation (r = 0.72) is more telling: respondents understand incentive misalignment and portfolio underinvestment as facets of the same problem.

3.2 Qualitative Patterns

Five themes emerged from the verbatim responses (see Appendix A). The most analytically significant is what I term the 'Day 1 Paradox': AWS's philosophy that every day should operate with start-up urgency is simultaneously the organisation's greatest cultural asset and its primary structural alibi. Because innovation is nominally 'everyone's job', it becomes no one's formally resourced responsibility. In Mintzberg's (1987) terms, AWS has built strategy-as-perspective — a deeply held identity around innovation — but has not translated this into strategy-as-plan at the field level. 'If you need a chief innovation officer, are you an innovative organisation?' (Respondent 14). In practice, this means innovation gets discussed at every all-hands but appears in no one's quarterly objectives. Christensen (2013) described exactly these conditions: innovation is systematically deprioritised under operational pressure.

The second theme, the UKI-to-Global disconnect, exposes a geographic fault line. Innovation infrastructure exists at corporate or EMEA level but does not translate locally, consistent with Teece's (2018) argument that dynamic capabilities are organisational rather than corporate properties. This creates a gap between the co-innovation value AWS promises customers and what AGS UKI can deliver. The third theme, fear of failure post-RIF (reduction in force), introduces a temporal dimension: 'fear of failure as a result of RIF and other factors over the last couple of years has diluted the desire for people to experiment and innovate' (Respondent 38). Innovation culture is not a stable state; it is fragile, susceptible to shocks unrelated to innovation itself. In an environment that has moved beyond VUCA to what Cascio (2020) terms BANI conditions (brittle, anxious, non-linear, incomprehensible), the post-RIF chill illustrates how quickly cultural capital can be destroyed by a single organisational event.

Competing priorities and resource cannibalisation reinforce the structural diagnosis: innovation initiatives such as GenAI Surge draw from the same resource pool as core operations. 'It can appear and feel to be a robbing Peter to pay Paul scenario if there isn't additional HC for backfill' (Respondent 38). This links to the Resource Allocation score of 3.02 and to Dodd and Favaro's (2006) tension between the whole and the parts: the corporate centre launches innovation programmes, but the field absorbs the cost. Finally, Working Backwards was recognised but unevenly applied: 'Nominally we have Working Backwards and an encouragement to be scrappy and iterative in innovation, but I'm unsure how often it's applied' (Respondent 9). The methodology exists as a search and selection routine (Tidd & Bessant, 2020) but is not consistently embedded as implementation practice, explaining the Process Management score of 3.34.

04
Analysis
Discussion

4.1 The Ambidexterity Paradox at AWS

The data make more sense through the lens of organisational ambidexterity (March, 1991; O'Reilly & Tushman, 2013). AWS's corporate architecture exemplifies the 'ambidextrous organisation'; Working Backwards (Bryar & Carr, 2021) is its most visible expression. The survey data suggest this does not cascade to AGS UKI. The field function operates in exploitation mode: incentives reward execution over experimentation, sales structures are designed for repeatability, and leadership defaults to measurable short-term outputs when the quarter is tight. These reinforce each other: the structure limits what incentives can reward, and leadership builds structures that reflect what it already measures. The strategic consequence: customer-facing teams, the people closest to emerging needs, cannot function as the innovation interfaces the external environment demands (see PESTLE, Appendix C).

Framed as a strategy problem, this is misallocation shaped by structural incentives, not resource scarcity. These are not parallel explanations: March (1991) identifies why the field remains locked in exploitation, Dodd and Favaro (2006) where the misallocation concentrates, and Teece (2018) what it costs: the inability to reconfigure assets where customer needs are shifting fastest. Dodd and Favaro frame this as the 'whole versus the parts' tension: the organisation possesses capabilities that are, in Barney's (1991) terms, valuable and rare but not organised for field deployment. Search and selection routines exist (Working Backwards, PRFAQ), but implementation and learning remain under-resourced locally: the ITP team is three UK-focused staff for 250 people, and the practical reality is a six-week queue for innovation support. Sull et al. (2015) identify precisely this pattern: strategy execution unravels not through wholesale failure but through coordination breakdowns between corporate intent and field-level resource allocation.

4.2 The Incentive Architecture Gap

Kerr's (1975) observation (that organisations reward A while hoping for B) is visible in every quarterly cycle: 'Besides stretch project work for promotion, there is no incentive for teams to innovate, as in many cases this detracts from day-to-day role and goals' (Respondent 19).

This challenges the Strategyzer model itself. The diagnostic treats dimensions as co-equal, but the data suggest otherwise: incentive alignment functions as a load-bearing element without which other dimensions cannot produce results. This interpretation warrants caution: the causality may run in both directions. Strong Process Management (3.34) and Innovation Skills (3.46) remain latent if the incentive architecture penalises innovation time. Osterwalder et al. (2020) acknowledge diagnosing readiness rather than predicting output, but failing to weight dimensions by structural dependency is a limitation the data expose. The Culture Map (Appendix D) visualises this hierarchy: formal blockers (incentive architecture) suppress behaviours that formal enablers (culture, process) are designed to produce. An alternative reading: the incentive deficit is symptomatic, the real constraint being strategic ambiguity about whether a field sales function should innovate (see Section 4.3). This ambiguity shares the characteristics of what Camillus (2008) terms a 'wicked problem': stakeholders disagree not just on the solution but on the problem definition itself.

The Rewards-to-Portfolio Management correlation (r = 0.72) supports systemic linkage. Nagji and Tuff (2012) propose a 70/20/10 innovation portfolio (core/adjacent/transformational); from my experience, AGS UKI operates closer to 90/10/0. That is a growth ceiling, not a growth strategy. Teece's (2018) argument that dynamic capabilities require executive commitment to reconfiguring assets reinforces the urgency: in GenAI, customers want to co-build and AGS UKI's best response is often a referral to a central team six time zones away.

4.3 Challenging the Thesis

The strongest counter-argument is that the Strategyzer diagnostic is the wrong tool for evaluating AWS. Several respondents made this point: 'Do we need such narrow definitions of organisational practices if innovation is fully ingrained?' (Respondent 14); 'We don't have innovation teams, but that's not to say it's outside official channels' (Respondent 16). The argument is that AWS has transcended formal structures because innovation is culturally embedded. A related argument holds that AGS UKI's role is execution and expecting a field sales function to innovate is a category error; on this reading, low scores reflect appropriate specialisation.

Both have force. Pisano (2019) warns against conflating absent formal structures with absent capability. If Leadership Principles substitute for formal incentives, the low Rewards score may be a measurement artefact; similarly, not every unit needs to explore if the corporate centre does so effectively. A stronger version of this argument notes that AWS remains among the most successful companies in the world — the current model clearly produces sufficient corporate-level innovation, and the diagnostic may simply be measuring a field function doing what it was designed to do.

Both counter-arguments have force, but the data do not support them. If innovation culture were genuinely embedded, we would expect convergence, not a 1.2-point IQR. The specialisation argument underestimates how rapidly enterprise customers now expect account teams to co-innovate; a field function that cannot explore cannot sense emerging needs. And the post-RIF environment has eroded the psychological safety that cultural embedding depends upon. As Edmondson (1999) demonstrated, psychological safety is a contingent state destroyed by threat-rigidity responses. The 2024 workforce reductions constitute precisely such a shock.

05
Action Plan
Recommendations

Each recommendation targets a specific constraint, but they are designed to work together. Klein and Knight (2005) identify key preconditions for innovation implementation, among them climate, management support, and resource availability, and AGS UKI fails on at least two locally. The hardest insight is one I have sat with throughout this analysis: the system requiring change, the incentive architecture, is controlled by precisely the leadership whose short-term competitive priorities it serves. That is not a critique; it is a description of a self-reinforcing constraint. Recommendations 1 and 5 require VP-level sponsorship; proposing them without naming this political dependency would be analytically dishonest.

Table 2: Prioritised Recommendations for Innovation Culture Improvement

Recommendation 1 is prioritised because the Rewards-to-Portfolio Management correlation (r = 0.72) suggests addressing the incentive deficit will have multiplicative effects. Even a modest 10% weighting in quarterly objectives shifts the signal from 'tolerated' to 'measured', though the design matters: poorly calibrated metrics risk performative compliance (Pisano, 2019). Recommendation 5 is marked 'immediate' because psychological safety erosion actively undermines all other interventions. Klein and Knight (2005) identify implementation climate as the strongest predictor of adoption, and Edmondson's (1999) research shows this is a team-level construct requiring team-by-team restoration. The logic is sequential, consistent with Kotter's (2012) emphasis on urgency before embedding change: restore safety (5), realign incentives (1–2), activate customer-facing capability (3–4).

06
Summary
Conclusion

AGS UKI possesses genuine cultural assets but a structural gap between aspiration and incentive architecture. The people are not the constraint; the systems governing daily behaviour are still optimised for exploitation, and innovation cannot be institutionalised within that architecture. The Strategyzer diagnostic has surfaced a concentrated deficit in Rewards and Incentives that qualitative data confirm is understood, structurally consequential, and increasingly misaligned with where AGS UKI needs to compete over the next two to three years.

The deeper paradox: the very culture that makes AWS exceptional at corporate-level innovation may inhibit it at the field level. 'Day 1' thinking distributes ownership so completely that no one accumulates the structural authority to protect exploration from operational pressure. The culture substitutes identity for architecture, and in a post-RIF environment, identity alone is insufficient. The recommendations propose a sequenced theory of change: restore safety, realign incentives, activate latent capability. Whether leadership acts is a strategic question, with competitive consequences that compound quarterly. What this study taught me is that a diagnostic measures the organisation and the analyst simultaneously, and the hardest findings to report are the ones you already knew but had not yet been asked to sign your name to.
