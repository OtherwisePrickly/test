01
Overview
Introduction

Amazon Web Services (AWS) operates in a market defined by accelerating uncertainty: generative AI is reshaping customer expectations, hyperscaler competition is intensifying, and enterprise buyers increasingly evaluate providers on co-innovation capacity, not merely infrastructure (Teece, 2018). This report examines innovation culture readiness in the AWS Global Sales (AGS) organisation within AWS UK and Ireland (UKI), a field sales function of approximately 250 professionals. A focused PESTLE analysis (Appendix C) confirms that the external environment increasingly demands innovation at the point of customer contact; the question is whether AGS UKI's internal culture can keep pace.

The study is motivated by a tension I observe daily as a Principal Account Manager. AWS's corporate narrative positions every employee as an innovator: 'Invent and Simplify' is recited in hiring and performance reviews, underpinned by Working Backwards (Bryar & Carr, 2021). Yet the operational reality of a quota-carrying sales function creates conditions that may constrain the very innovation the culture claims to enable. Pisano (2019) argues innovative cultures require uncomfortable counterparts including rigorous discipline; Tidd and Bessant (2020) reinforce this, identifying dedicated routines for search, selection, implementation, and learning as preconditions. What remains unclear is whether AGS UKI possesses the architecture to convert cultural aspiration into competitive practice.

Using a Strategyzer Innovation Culture Readiness diagnostic (Osterwalder et al., 2020) administered to 41 respondents, findings are analysed through organisational ambidexterity (O'Reilly & Tushman, 2013), dynamic capabilities (Teece, 2018), and the structural tensions of managing exploitation and exploration simultaneously (Dodd & Favaro, 2006). The argument that emerges from the data is specific: AGS UKI's innovation deficit sits in its incentive architecture, and the strategic consequences, for customer value creation and the organisation's ability to compete in a market moving faster than its internal systems, are compounding.

02
Research Design
Methodology

The Strategyzer Innovation Culture Readiness survey (Osterwalder et al., 2020) was deployed digitally to AGS UKI in January 2026. The instrument evaluates nine dimensions across three categories: Leadership Support (Strategic Guidance, Resource Allocation, Portfolio Management), Organisational Design (Legitimacy and Power, Bridge to the Core, Rewards and Incentives), and Innovation Practice (Innovation Tools, Process Management, Innovation Skills). Each dimension uses a five-point Likert scale with anchored descriptors, supplemented with qualitative open-text questions enabling triangulation between what respondents score and what they explain.

Forty-one responses were received (approximately 16% of the AGS UKI population), including individual contributors, team leads, senior managers, and ITP (Innovation and Transformation Programmes) team members. The survey was anonymised, preventing stratified analysis by role or tenure, and the convenience sample means findings are indicative rather than generalisable. My insider position gives the analysis contextual depth but introduces confirmation bias risk, which I have tried to navigate transparently.

Quantitative data were analysed using descriptive statistics and Pearson correlations. Qualitative responses were subjected to thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase approach (Appendix A). The diagnostic warrants critical appraisal: it lacks psychometric validation and its five-point scale introduces central tendency bias. More fundamentally, it captures how people experience innovation culture through self-report, not necessarily how the organisation performs innovation as a process of search, selection, implementation, and value creation (Tidd & Bessant, 2020). I treat it as a problem-diagnosis tool consistent with Rumelt's (2022) challenge-led approach: identifying where the organisation believes its constraints lie is itself strategically useful. The results were additionally mapped to Strategyzer's Culture Map framework (Appendix D) to visualise the relationship between outcomes, behaviours, and the formal and informal enablers and blockers shaping AGS UKI's innovation culture.

Quantitative & Qualitative Results
Findings
3.23
The overall mean across nine dimensions places AGS UKI in the 'Emerging' band, but that aggregate obscures the real story: significant inter-dimensional variance revealing an organisation with the raw materials for innovation but no mechanism to convert them into output.

The deficit is concentrated: AGS UKI has invested in capability but has not aligned its incentive architecture to activate it. The constraint is internal, which means it is addressable.

The interquartile range in individual means (2.67–3.89), with scores ranging from 5.00 to 1.33, reflects not individual disagreement but a structural access problem. AGS UKI functions as multiple sub-organisations within one firm.

Strategic Guidance (3.46) and Legitimacy and Power (3.49) suggest leadership intent and formal authority to innovate are present; the organisation has permission but not incentive. Innovation Tools (3.12) clusters at the midpoint, with 46% scoring exactly 3, indicating tools exist but are not salient enough to function as enablers. The correlation analysis shows structural relationships. Bridge to the Core and Innovation Skills (r = 0.73) suggests collaboration and talent capability are linked. The Portfolio Management and Rewards correlation (r = 0.72) is more telling: respondents understand incentive misalignment and portfolio underinvestment as facets of the same problem.

Five themes emerged from the verbatim responses (see Appendix A). Competing priorities and resource cannibalisation emerged as a persistent pattern: innovation initiatives such as GenAI Surge draw from the same resource pool as core operations rather than receiving additional investment. This links to the Resource Allocation score of 3.02 and to Dodd and Favaro's (2006) tension between the whole and the parts. Finally, Working Backwards was recognised but unevenly applied, explaining the Process Management score of 3.34.

04
Analysis
Discussion

4.1 The Ambidexterity Paradox at AWS

The data make more sense through the lens of organisational ambidexterity (March, 1991; O'Reilly & Tushman, 2013). AWS's corporate architecture exemplifies the 'ambidextrous organisation'; Working Backwards (Bryar & Carr, 2021) is its most visible expression. The survey data suggest this does not cascade to AGS UKI. The field function operates in exploitation mode: incentives reward execution over experimentation, sales structures are designed for repeatability, and leadership defaults to measurable short-term outputs when the quarter is tight.

Framed as a strategy problem, this is misallocation shaped by structural incentives, not resource scarcity. March (1991) identifies why the field remains locked in exploitation, Dodd and Favaro (2006) where the misallocation concentrates, and Teece (2018) what it costs: the inability to reconfigure assets where customer needs are shifting fastest. AGS UKI's exploitation-dominant mode also reflects what Brandenburger (2019) describes as the exclusion of creative strategic thinking from execution-focused structures. Search and selection routines exist (Working Backwards, PRFAQ), but implementation and learning remain under-resourced locally. The ITP team is three UK-focused staff for 250 people.

2.39
The only 'Developing' dimension and 0.63 points below the next-lowest, Rewards & Incentives represents a fundamental misalignment between what the organisation values (innovation) and what it rewards (execution).

4.2 The Incentive Architecture Gap

Kerr's (1975) observation that organisations reward A while hoping for B is visible in every quarterly cycle. This challenges the Strategyzer model itself. The diagnostic treats dimensions as co-equal, but the data suggest otherwise: incentive alignment functions as a load-bearing element without which other dimensions cannot produce results. Strong Process Management (3.34) and Innovation Skills (3.46) remain latent if the incentive architecture penalises innovation time.

The Rewards-to-Portfolio Management correlation (r = 0.72) supports systemic linkage. Nagji and Tuff (2012) propose a 70/20/10 innovation portfolio; AGS UKI operates closer to 90/10/0. That is a growth ceiling, not a growth strategy. Teece's (2018) argument that dynamic capabilities require executive commitment to reconfiguring assets reinforces the urgency.

4.3 Challenging the Thesis

The strongest counter-argument is that the Strategyzer diagnostic is the wrong tool for evaluating AWS. The argument is that AWS has transcended formal structures because innovation is culturally embedded. A related argument holds that AGS UKI's role is execution and expecting a field sales function to innovate is a category error.

Both counter-arguments have force, but the data do not support them. If innovation culture were genuinely embedded, we would expect convergence, not a 1.2-point IQR. The specialisation argument underestimates how rapidly enterprise customers now expect account teams to co-innovate. And the post-RIF environment has eroded the psychological safety that cultural embedding depends upon. As Edmondson (1999) demonstrated, psychological safety is a contingent state destroyed by threat-rigidity responses.

05
Action Plan
Recommendations

Each recommendation targets a specific constraint, but they are designed to work together. Klein and Knight (2005) identify key preconditions for innovation implementation, among them climate, management support, and resource availability, and AGS UKI fails on at least two locally. The logic is sequential, consistent with Kotter's (2012) emphasis on urgency and coalition before embedding change: restore safety (5), realign incentives (1–2), activate latent capability (3–4).

06
Summary
Conclusion

AGS UKI possesses genuine cultural assets but a structural gap between aspiration and incentive architecture. The people are not the constraint; the systems governing daily behaviour are still optimised for exploitation, and innovation cannot be institutionalised within that architecture. The Strategyzer diagnostic has surfaced a concentrated deficit in Rewards and Incentives that qualitative data confirm is understood, structurally consequential, and increasingly misaligned with where AGS UKI needs to compete over the next two to three years.

The deeper paradox is this: the very culture that makes AWS exceptional at corporate-level innovation may inhibit it at the field level. 'Day 1' thinking distributes ownership so completely that no one accumulates the structural authority to protect exploration from operational pressure. The culture substitutes identity for architecture, and in a post-RIF environment where psychological safety has been eroded, identity alone is insufficient. The recommendations propose a sequenced theory of change: restore safety, realign incentives, activate latent capability. Whether leadership acts is not a research question but a strategic one, with competitive consequences that compound with each quarter of inaction.
